# THE SHAVIAN ALPHABET

The Shavian alphabet is used to represent *sounds* in English, rather than individual letters (though many letters map directly to the equivalent letter in English).  "Normal" spoken English contains 40 unique sounds, each of which is represented by a unique Shavian letter.  In addition, Shavian contains 8 compound letters which are used to represent common combinations of sounds.

## Additional Notes

* A letter can be either **tall** (*higher* on the line) or **deep** (*lower* on the line).  Tall letters are unvoiced---there is no vibration of the vocal chords.  Deep letters on the other hand are voiced---the vocal chords vibrate. For example, compare the sounds of ğ‘• (tall) and ğ‘Ÿ (deep).
* The letter ğ‘§ looks like part of the letter 'e' which can help to remember its sound.
* Note that the letter ğ‘± for **AI**m is almost the same as the letter ğ‘§ for **E**dge, except with an added line---this represents the added 'ee' sound after the ğ‘§ sound, which lengthens the sound.  This concept will recur in the case of other letters.
* The letter ğ‘² for **EYE**s (cf. ğ‘¨ for **A**sh) follows the same system as ğ‘§ and ğ‘± above, where the extra line adds the 'ee' sound after the ğ‘¨ sound.
* Note the similarity between the letters ğ‘¥ and ğ‘¯.  To remember which is which, the following may be helpful.  The sound ğ‘¥ is made with the lips at the *front* of the mouth, whereas ğ‘¯ is made with the tongue pointing *back*.
* ğ‘‘ and ğ‘› are also similar.  Think of ğ‘‘ as pointing to the **T**op, and ğ‘› as pointing **D**own.
* ğ‘¤ and ğ‘® can be remembered using your **L**eft (ğ‘¤ğ‘§ğ‘“ğ‘‘) and **R**ight (ğ‘®ğ‘²ğ‘‘) hands and forming them each into a cup shape: when viewed the corresponding hand will form the corresponding shape.
* The compound letter ğ‘¼ allows pronunciation however the speaker sees fit---that is, it allows for *different accents*.  For example, many British accents wouldn't pronounce the final "r" sound in "flavour", but an American might.
* In Received Pronunciation, ğ‘· and ğ‘¹ sound the same---this can differ for speakers with an American accent
